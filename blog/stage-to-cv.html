From stage to CV.
Why a Blastbeat year outranks an internship.

Recruiters say they want “experience.” They actually want evidence of judgement under stakes. A Blastbeat year is, line by line, exactly that. Here’s how to put it at the top of your CV.

BEBlastbeat Education · 4 April 2026 · 9 min read · Reviewed by recruiting partners

The single best line on a 19-year-old’s CV isn’t “intern, summer 2025, mostly making coffee.” It’s “CFO of a real youth event business that ran an event in front of 350 people, kept the books, returned a R21,000 profit, and ring-fenced 25% for a beach-clean.”

Recruiters know it. Hiring managers know it. The only thing missing has been a clean way to write that line down. The Blastbeat Licence is now that line.

What recruiters actually screen for

The major employer surveys converge on roughly the same shortlist. The WEF Future of Jobs Report (2025) lists the top five “most in-demand” skills for 2025–2030 as analytical thinking, creative thinking, resilience & flexibility, leadership & social influence, and technological literacy.1

The CIPD UK Skills Outlook (2024) found that 71% of UK employers cite “evidence of personal initiative” as more important than grades for entry-level roles.2 A grade tells them what you scored. An ESE year tells them what you decided to do.

The four things a Blastbeat year proves

Better than an internship. Almost always.

1. You’ve carried a P&L

Not in a workbook. In real life. With your name on the spreadsheet and a real bank balance to show for it. The 2024 cohort medians were R44,820 in revenue, R23,400 in costs, and R21,420 in net profit per team. Most adults haven’t made that calculation themselves yet.

2. You’ve worked under deadline pressure with consequences

Event week is the most stressful work week most students will have until they’re 25. Recruiters love this story because it’s rare and provable.

3. You’ve managed real adults

Sponsors. Suppliers. Venue managers. Security. The hospitality vendor who didn’t show up. Negotiating with adults at sixteen is a hard skill, and it shows on Day One in any role.

4. You’ve delivered, not described

Most internship CVs are full of verbs that translate to “watched” (“shadowed”, “observed”, “assisted”). Your Blastbeat year is full of verbs that translate to “ran” (CEO’d, sold, designed, produced, banked, reported).

How to write the line

Three lines on a CV. Tested with five recruiting partners. This is the format they prefer:

CFO — Blastbeat ESE Programme (Class A · MACC)
Mitchells Plain Comprehensive · Cape Town · 2026
· Carried R44.8K event budget; closed at R21.4K net profit (47.7% margin)
· Banked, reconciled and reported quarterly to a school governance committee
· Verifiable licence: BBE-LIC-2026-MAC-00142 (verify at blastbeat.education/verify)

The verification line is the secret weapon. It’s rare for a high-school CV to carry a verifiable credential. It signals seriousness without saying anything else.

What to talk about in interviews

Three stories carry the entire interview round. Have one polished version of each. The recruiters we work with say they’ve never seen a Blastbeat alumnus run out of stories to tell.

The crisis story

Pick the moment in your event week when something went wrong. The vendor didn’t show. The lighting broke. The headline act was late. Tell what happened, what you decided, and what the result was. Two minutes max.

The financial story

Pick a single line in your P&L where the maths nearly tipped the wrong way. Print costs, ticket-pricing, the contingency line. Tell the story of how you made the call. Numbers, not adjectives.

The team story

Pick a moment where two team members disagreed and you helped resolve it. Bonus points if you weren’t the CEO. Most interviewers haven’t had to do this until their late twenties.

  1. Verifiable digital credentials are spreading. The EU Digital Wallet, Norwegian state diplomas, and a growing number of professional licences all use the W3C VC standard. The Blastbeat Licence rides that same standard. Read the case for it.

  3. Employers are increasingly comfortable with non-traditional pathways. Two-thirds of large UK employers now accept “skills-first” entry routes (CIPD 2024).2

The Blastbeat Licence is designed to ride those trends. We want a 16-year-old’s real-world ESE result to mean something at a job interview ten years later.
