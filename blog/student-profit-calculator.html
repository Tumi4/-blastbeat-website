For Students · The Maths

R0 to R45K.
The maths of a Blastbeat year.

Most students think running an event needs a parent’s money. The honest maths says the opposite. Here’s exactly how a 14-person team starts with R0 and ends a year holding real money.

BEBlastbeat Education · 8 April 2026 · 8 min read · Verified by 2024 cohort financials

Imagine you’re fifteen. You’ve got fourteen classmates, a school hall on a Friday in October, and zero rand in the bank. The challenge is simple: turn that into a profitable event by the end of November. The maths is more achievable than you think.

This is the calculator we walk every Blastbeat team through in their first week. Real numbers, real ranges, real outcomes from past cohorts.

Step 1: The starting cap table

Every Blastbeat team starts with R0. No school money. No parent money. No personal savings. The first cash that lands in the team’s account is the first money the team has earned.

That feels like a constraint. It’s actually the entire engine. When you have to earn the float for your event, you have to sell tickets and find sponsors before you spend a rand. That sequence — income before expenditure — is the most important business lesson the year teaches.

Total cost of running the event: ~R25,000. That’s the number the team has to beat.

Step 3: Revenue lines

Three streams of revenue are available to a Blastbeat team. Most teams use all three.

Ticket revenue

A 350-person school event with tickets at R75–R120 generates between R26,000 and R42,000 if the team sells 80% capacity. Most teams aim for capacity 80% as a planning baseline.

Sponsorship

Local sponsors — small businesses, parents’ companies, local stores — typically contribute R2,000–R10,000 each in cash or in-kind value. Most teams secure 2–5 sponsors. Median sponsorship pulled in 2024 was R14,500 per team.

Concessions

Soft drinks, snacks and light hospitality at the event. Margin is tight but adds R3,000–R6,000 to a strong event.

Step 4: The 75/25 split

Once costs are paid, the profit splits. 75% goes to the team — to share, save, or reinvest. 25% goes to a climate project the team designs themselves.

75% — the team
Splits among the 14 students, or saves to fund their next event.
25% — climate project
Ring-fenced. Team designs and reports on the project.

Step 5: A worked example

Let’s walk through a realistic 2024 cohort case. Mitchells Plain Comprehensive’s FootBeat team in October 2024:

  • Tickets sold: 312 at R85 = R26,520
  • Local sponsorship (3 sponsors): R14,000
  • Concessions: R4,300
  • Total revenue: R44,820
  • Cost of running event: R23,400
  • Net profit: R21,420
  • To team (75%): R16,065
  • To climate project (25%): R5,355

The 14-student team chose to split half of the team share among themselves (R574 each, taxable as “own work” under the relevant SARS schedules) and reinvest the other half in their finale-event preparation. The R5,355 climate share went to a coastal-clean-up project on Strandfontein beach.

What students learn from the maths

The honest answer is that they don’t learn the maths. The maths isn’t hard. They learn three things the maths only reveals:

Cash flow vs profit. A team that sells R44K in tickets but spent R23K to get there has profited R21K. Until they internalise that distinction, they’ll feel they’re “winning” long before they actually are.

Risk vs return. Spending R7,500 on a venue you can’t fill is a problem. Spending R3,000 on marketing that fills the venue is the answer. Risk is what you allocate before you know.

Discipline. The 25% climate ring-fence is non-negotiable. Teams cannot dip into it. Learning to leave money alone is, frankly, more important than learning to earn it.

“The first time I saw the bank balance after we’d paid all the bills, I cried. We had R21,000 in the account. We’d started the year with nothing.”

— CFO, 2024 cohort, Mitchells Plain
